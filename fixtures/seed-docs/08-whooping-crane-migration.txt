# Whooping Crane Migration Across the Platte Corridor

Each spring whooping cranes stage along the Platte River, probing wet meadows
for crabs, frogs, and waste grain. Biologists track breeding pairs with
transmitters, count juveniles, and fight for water flows that keep roosting
sandbars wide enough to deter predators. The birds stand nearly five feet tall,
white with black wingtips, and bugle calls that carry across marsh at dusk.

Captive-rearing programs once taught ultra-light aircraft routes so captive
chicks could learn a safe migration path. Habitat loss in wintering marshes
still threatens the flock; conservation easements try to keep stopover wetlands
from becoming subdivisions. Birders travel hundreds of miles for a glimpse of
a species that nearly vanished in the twentieth century.

Nothing in this file concerns construction sites, load charts, or steel hooks.
When we say crane, we mean the endangered migratory bird and its wetland
ecology—not a machine that lifts girders.
