# Cold-Pressed Cider and the Heirloom Orchard

In late September the hillside orchard turns sharp with the smell of fallen fruit.
Crews walk the rows at dawn, shaking limbs so ripe apples drop into padded tarps
rather than bruising on hard ground. The best cider stock is never the glossy
supermarket specimen; it is a tart heirloom with thick skin, high tannin, and a
core that resists soft rot after nights of frost.

The farm presses within forty-eight hours of picking. Juice is racked into
stainless tanks, allowed a quiet primary ferment, then rested on fine lees until
the finished cider tastes of orchard floor, pear skin, and wild yeast rather than
sugar water. Grafting workshops in spring teach visitors how to join a scion of
an antique variety onto a hardy rootstock so the tree survives clay soil and
bitter winters.

Nothing in this document concerns phones, laptops, app stores, Cupertino campuses,
or quarterly earnings. When we say apple, we mean the edible fruit hanging from
a living tree—picked, pressed, fermented, and shared at harvest tables under
yellow leaves.
