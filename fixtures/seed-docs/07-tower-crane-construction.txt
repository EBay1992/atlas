# Tower Crane Rigging on a High-Rise Jobsite

On a downtown pour day the tower crane climbs with the building, its mast
bolted to climbing frames as floors rise. The operator in the cab reads radio
calls from the tagline crew, swings a load of rebar over the deck, and waits
for the signal before lowering the hook. Riggers inspect wire rope, anti-two-
block devices, and outrigger pads when a mobile unit works the perimeter.

Project managers schedule concrete buckets between steel deliveries; wind
limits can idle the boom for hours. Manufacturers publish load charts that
trade radius for capacity: farther out means fewer tons. Failures are rare
but catastrophic, so daily checklists cover slew brakes, limit switches, and
counterweight pins.

This document is about heavy construction machinery that lifts steel and
concrete. It is not about migratory birds, nesting grounds, or wildlife
photography. When we say crane, we mean the engineered lifting machine on a
jobsite, not an animal with long legs.
