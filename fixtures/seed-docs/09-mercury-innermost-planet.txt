# Mercury’s Solar Year and the Innermost Orbit

Astronomers describe Mercury as the scorched innermost planet, locked in a
3:2 spin-orbit resonance that makes a solar day longer than its year. Daytime
temperatures soar above 400°C; nights plunge toward −170°C because the world
has almost no atmosphere to hold heat. Radar mapping revealed a battered
surface of basins and scarps, while spacecraft measured a large iron core that
helps generate a weak magnetic field.

Observers still chase the planet in twilight, low on the horizon, never far
from the Sun’s glare. Transit events—when the tiny disk crosses the solar face—
remain rare public spectacles. Models of solar system formation ask why such
a dense body sits so close to the star, and whether early giant impacts stripped
its mantle.

This document is planetary science. It is not about thermometers, liquid metal
in glass tubes, toxic vapors, or dental amalgams. When we say Mercury, we mean
the planet nearest the Sun—not the chemical element.
